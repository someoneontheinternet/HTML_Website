The Arctics

Grade 9 Social Project

Introduction

The Arctic Region is among the uttermost interesting places on Earth. The region is composed of three territories: Yukon, NorthWest Territory, Nunavut confederated on July 1870. The region contains a large amount of diversity in terrains, climates, culture, and wildlife. With the current population of around 75,249, the arctic region is only 8% of the population in Vancouver. However, with the region mainly focusing on mining as one of the most important aspects of their economy, the region is still productive. The region also has a contrasting climate since it is situated inland with freezing Atlantic air blowing across the region. Most of the time, the temperature is pretty extreme reaching -40C in the winter, while 25C in the Summer. During the summer time, the territory can attract ten’s of thousands of Tourist all around the world to view the beautiful scenery. Agriculture is not a huge part of the economy since permafrost covers a huge area of land rendering the upper north part extremely difficult to farm. Overall, the Arctics is still one of the most interesting places.


Geography

Terrains

The terrain of the arctic consists of many geographical features, including mountains, low-lying hills, some marshes (Hudson Bay and northern Winnipeg), tundra, packed ice, glaciers, many freshwater lakes, and densely packed forests, which are mainly concentrated in the south. The rock of the arctic consists of mainly sedimentary and igneous rock. Intrusive and metamorphic can also be present. Mountains are mainly present on the “mainland” part of the territories, while the tundra covers most of the northern part of the territories, such as Baffin Island. Since over time many minerals have formed due to volcanic eruptions and lava flowing millions, even billions of years ago, Minerals include gold, zinc, iron, lead, quartz, diamonds and other valuable minerals. While some of the geography can be interesting, some can (to be honest) be boring. The tundra consist of almost nothing, being just low-lying hills that have rock and have permafrost, which is permanent frost that thaws a bit over the summer. Overall the terrain can be interesting, but can be boring also.

Vegetation

The vegetation of the arctic is barer as you go north. The vegetation is mainly concentrated in the south. But, don’t get me wrong here. The vegetation consists of many trees, bushes, berries, grass, moss, and much more, especially trees. The trees that subside the forests in the arctic are mainly coniferous. Such trees that are commonly found are pine, spruce, cedar, fir, and many more. Some deciduous trees that are seldom found are birch, mountain ash, and the most common flour is the fireweed, which grows in fertile soil left behind by lava. The most common moss is lichen, which grows on rocks found in the tundra. While this sounds bland, lichen is an important contributor to diets of local wildlife. Overall, the vegetation has a big variety, which is good for the ecosystem.

Wildlife

While the terrain and vegetation of the arctic can be limited, the wildlife is not! There are many animals that reside in the arctic, from mammals to birds to even bacteria! Mammals include reindeer, polar bear, arctic fox, narwhal, walrus, seal, ox, moose, orca, and more. Birds include the snowy owl, Yellow-Billed Loon, Rock Ptarmigan, Peregrine Falcon, buff-breasted Sandpiper, steller’s eider, snow goose, ruddy turnstone, rock ptarmigan, and more. Fish include the Atlantic cod, salmon, trout, char, American eel, shrimp, krill, Greenland halibut, capelin, common sea snail, haddock, viviparous eelpout, pacific herring, pacific sleeper shark, nine-spine stickleback, two- horn sculpin, and pink salmon. Overall, A LOT OF ANIMALS live in the arctic, and rely on the topography and vegetation necessary to live.
